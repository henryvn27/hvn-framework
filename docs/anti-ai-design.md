# Anti-Generic Design

AI-generated interfaces often look generic because they optimize for surface polish instead of product fit. The result is a familiar mix of gradients, soft cards, icon grids, oversized hero copy, and decorative elements that do not clarify the user's task.

## Signals To Watch

- Gradient-heavy SaaS visuals without product relevance
- Purple or blue blob aesthetics
- Repetitive three-column feature-card grids
- Excessive icon-in-circle templates
- Generic startup hero copy patterns
- Uniformly rounded, over-smoothed components
- Decorative clutter replacing real hierarchy
- Visually expensive but strategically empty sections
- Stock-like imagery or abstract illustrations where real product context is needed

## Calibration Questions

Before building, answer:

- What is the user's primary job on this screen?
- What should the first viewport prove?
- What visual direction fits the product category?
- Which generic patterns are specifically banned?
- What real content, data, screenshots, product state, or domain detail should drive the layout?
- What component decisions support hierarchy and trust?

## Design Direction

Distinct design comes from product specificity. Use the audience, domain, content shape, constraints, and actual workflows to choose layout, density, typography, imagery, motion, and interaction patterns.

## Review

Design review should flag templated patterns, generic visual tropes, incoherent decoration, weak information hierarchy, and first impressions that feel indistinct or untrustworthy.
